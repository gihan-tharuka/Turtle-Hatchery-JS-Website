Turtle categories in Sri Lanka

Turtles are fascinating creatures that have been around for over 200 million years. They are known for their unique shells, which provide them with protection and stability, and their slow and steady movements. Turtles are found all over the world, living in a variety of habitats including oceans, rivers, lakes, forests, and deserts.

over 300 different species, turtles come in a wide range of sizes, shapes, and colors. Despite their long history and diversity, turtles face numerous threats to their survival including habitat loss, poaching, and climate change. As a result, many efforts are being made to protect and conserve these ancient and important species.

Green Turtle

Green sea turtle is named for the green color of the fat under its shell. (In some areas, the Pacific green turtle is also called the black sea turtle.) They are easily distinguished from other sea turtles because they have a single pair of prefrontal scales (scales in front of its eyes), rather than two pairs as found on other sea turtles. Head is small and blunt with a serrated jaw.

Diet of the Green turtle changes significantly during its life. When less than 8 to 10 inches in length eat worms, young crustaceans, aquatic insects, grasses and algae. Once green turtles reach 8 to 10 inches in length, they mostly eat sea grass and algae, the only sea turtle that is strictly herbivorous as an adult.

Green turtles nest at intervals of about every 2 years, with wide year-to-year fluctuations in numbers of nesting females.

Green Turtle

The Green turtles mainly stay near the coastline and around islands and live in bays and protected shores, especially in areas with seagrass beds. Rarely are they observed in the open ocean.

They the most commonly found turtle in Sri Lanka and one of the largest known sea turtles. Green turtles nest all year round in Sri Lanka, with the highest abundance reported in Kosgoda (north of Galle) and Rekawa (near Tangalle). The best time to see them nest is between January and March.

Hawksbill Turtle

Hawksbill is named for its narrow head and hawk-like beak. The hawksbill is one of the smaller sea turtles. Head is narrow and has 2 pairs of prefrontal scales (scales in front of its eyes). Jaw is not serrated. Carapace is bony without ridges and has large, overlapping scutes (scales) present and has 4 lateral scutes. Carapace is elliptical in shape. Flippers have 2 claws. The carapace is orange, brown or yellow and hatchlings are mostly brown with pale blotches on scutes.

It is listed as critically endangered by the World Conservation Union as a result of poachers seeking its beautiful ‘tortoise-shell’, which is commercially valuable, human consumption of tortoise flesh & eggs in addition to the reptile getting wedged in fishing gear.

Its habitat is mainly coral reefs and shallow lagoons where it feeds on sponges, fish, sea anemones and jellyfish amongst others. They Nest at intervals of 2 to 4 years. Nests between 3 to 6 times per season. Lays an average 160 eggs in each nest. Eggs incubate for about 60 days.

Hawksbill Turtle

One of the best places to see Hawksbill turtles in Sri Lanka is the Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project, located on the west coast of the island. The project is dedicated to the conservation of sea turtles, including the Hawksbill turtle, and visitors can participate in turtle watching tours or volunteer with the project to help with conservation efforts.

Other places where Hawksbill turtles can be occasionally spotted in Sri Lanka include the beaches of the Yala National Park and the Rekawa Turtle Conservation Project in the Southern Province.

Olive Ridley Turtle

Olive ridley is named for its olive green colored shell.It's head is quite small. Carapace is bony without ridges and has large scutes (scales) present. Carapace has 6 or more lateral scutes and is nearly circular and smooth. Its body is deeper than the very similar Kemp’s Ridley sea turtle. Both the front and rear flippers have 1 or 2 visible claws.

The adults measure 2 to 2.5 feet (62-70 cm) in carapace length. Adult turtles weigh between 77 and 100 pounds (35-45 kg).The Olive Ridley feeds mainly on jellyfish, shrimp, mollusks and fish and has an average lifespan of 50 years.

The Olive Ridley turtles nest every year in mass synchronized nestings called arribadas (Spanish for “arrival”). Only the Kemp’s ridley also nests this way. Nests 2 times each season. An average clutch size is over 110 eggs which require a 52 to 58 day incubation period.

Olive Ridley Turtle

They are generally found in coastal bays and estuaries, but can be very oceanic over some parts of its range. They typically forage off shore in surface waters or dive to depths of 500 feet (150 m) to feed on bottom dwelling crustaceans. The olive ridley inhabits tropical and subtropical waters of the Pacific, Indian and Atlantic Oceans.

One of the best places to see Olive Ridley turtles in Sri Lanka is at the Rekawa Turtle Conservation Project in the town of Rekawa, located in the Southern Province. The project is dedicated to the conservation of five species of sea turtles that come to nest on the beaches of Sri Lanka, including the Olive Ridley turtle.

Loggerhead Turtle

Loggerhead turtle is named for its exceptionally large head. Head is very large with heavy strong jaws. Carapace is bony without ridges and has large, non-overlapping, rough scutes (scales) present with 5 lateral scute. Carapace is heart shaped. Front flippers are short and thick with 2 claws, while the rear flippers can have 2 or 3 claws.

The Loggerhead turtles are primarily carnivorous and feed mostly on shellfish that live on the bottom of the ocean. They eat horseshoe crabs, clams, mussels, and other invertebrates. Their powerful jaw muscles help them to easily crush the shellfish.

They nests at intervals of 2 to 4 years. They lay 3 to 6 nests per season, approximately 12 to 14 days apart. Lays average of between 100 to 126 eggs in each nest. Eggs incubate for about 60 days.

Loggerhead Turtle

Loggerhead turtles are a rare species in Sri Lanka and sightings of them are very rare. The majority of sea turtle conservation projects and turtle watching tours in Sri Lanka focus on the Olive Ridley, Green and Leatherback turtles.

However, there is a chance of seeing loggerhead turtles on the southern coast of Sri Lanka, especially in the waters around Galle, Mirissa, and Tangalle. Visitors can try going on a whale and dolphin watching tour or hire a local fisherman to take them out to sea for a chance to spot loggerhead turtles.

Leatherback Turtle

The Leatherback turtle is named for its unique shell which is composed of a layer of thin, tough, rubbery skin, strengthened by thousands of tiny bone plates that makes it look “leathery”. Head has a deeply notched upper jaw with 2 cusps. The leatherback is the only sea turtle that lacks a hard shell. Its carapace is large, elongated and flexible with 7 distinct ridges running the length of the animal.

Leatherbacks have delicate, scissor-like jaws. Their jaws would be damaged by anything other than a diet of soft-bodied animals, so they feed almost exclusively on jellyfish. It is remarkable that this large, active animal can survive on a diet of jellyfish, which are composed mostly of water and appear to be a poor source of nutrients.

They are primarily found in the open ocean, as far north as Alaska and as far south as the southern tip of Africa, though recent satellite tracking research indicates that leatherbacks feed in areas just offshore.

Leatherback Turtle

Leatherback turtles are the most widely distributed of all sea turtles. Found world wide with the largest north and south range of all the sea turtle species.

Some of the best places to try and see leatherback turtles in Sri Lanka include the beaches of the Yala National Park, the Bundala National Park, and the beaches around the town of Pottuvil in the Eastern Province.
